CHAPTER 01

What is Jainism?

Jainism is one of the world's oldest religions, originating in ancient India around the 6th century BCE (or even earlier). The word "Jain" comes from the Sanskrit word "Jina" — meaning conqueror. Not someone who conquers other people, but someone who conquers their own mind, desires, anger, greed, and ego.

A Jain is a follower of the Jinas — spiritually liberated souls who have conquered all inner enemies and attained perfect knowledge. Jainism teaches that every living soul has the potential to become divine and free — not through God's grace, but through one's own effort, self-discipline, and non-violence.

🌟 The Core Message

Ahimsa Paramo Dharma — Non-violence is the highest religion. Every soul is divine and capable of liberation. The universe operates on justice (karma), not divine will. There is no creator God in Jainism — only perfect souls who serve as guides.

CHAPTER 02

History & Origins

Jainism is believed to be an eternal religion — not created at a specific point in time. Jains believe that every cosmic cycle produces 24 great teachers (Tirthankaras) who rediscover and share the truth. We are currently in the downward phase of the current cosmic cycle.

PRE-HISTORY (VERY ANCIENT)

Rishabhadeva — 1st Tirthankara

The first Tirthankara of the current age. Mentioned even in the Hindu Puranas and Vedas as a great sage. Some scholars link him to pre-Vedic civilization (Indus Valley).

~877 BCE

Parshvanatha — 23rd Tirthankara

Lived 100 years, renounced worldly life at age 30. Taught 4 vows: Non-violence, Truth, Non-stealing, Non-possession. Historical figure acknowledged widely.

599–527 BCE

Mahavira — 24th Tirthankara

The most recent Tirthankara. Born a prince in Vaishali (Bihar). Attained enlightenment (Kevala Jnana) at 42 and taught for 30 years. Added the 5th vow of Brahmacharya (celibacy).

~4th–3rd BCE

Division: Digambara & Shvetambara

After a great famine, Jain community split — Digambara monks believed in complete nudity, Shvetambara wore white robes. Both follow the same core teachings.

~2nd BCE – 2nd CE

Golden Age of Jainism

Jainism flourished under various kings. Chandragupta Maurya became a Jain monk. Jain scholars contributed enormously to mathematics, astronomy, logic, and literature.

10th–12th CE

Great Temples Built

Dilwara Temples (1031 CE), Gomateshwara statue (981 CE), Ranakpur temples built — masterpieces of Indian architecture and Jain devotion.

TODAY

~6 Million Jains Worldwide

Jains are a small but highly educated, prosperous, and peaceful community. Spread across India, USA, UK, East Africa, and beyond. Major contributors to Indian business, culture, and philosophy.

CHAPTER 04

Lord Mahavira — The Great Hero

Birth & Early Life

Born on the 13th day of Chaitra month (around 599 BCE) in Kundagram (present-day Vaishali, Bihar) to King Siddhartha and Queen Trishala. His birth name was Vardhamana — meaning "ever-increasing fortune."

The Renunciation

At age 30, despite being a prince with a loving family, Vardhamana gave up all worldly comfort. He pulled out his hair in five handfuls (a Jain initiation ritual) and became a wandering monk — with no clothes, no food storage, no home.

12 Years of Austerity

He underwent incredible hardships for 12 years — enduring heat, cold, insects, abuse from villagers, and extreme hunger. He maintained absolute non-violence and equanimity throughout.

Enlightenment (Kevala Jnana)

At age 42, meditating under a Sal tree on the banks of the Rijupalika river, he attained Kevala Jnana — omniscient perfect knowledge. He became a Jina — the Conqueror — and the title Mahavira (Great Hero) was given.

Liberation (Nirvana)

At age 72, on Diwali night (~527 BCE) at Pawapuri (Bihar), Mahavira attained Nirvana — final liberation from the cycle of birth and death. This night is celebrated as Diwali by Jains to this day.

Namo Arihantānam | Namo Siddhānam | Namo Āyariyānam | Namo Uvajjhāyānam | Namo Loe Savva-Sāhūnam
Meaning: I bow to the Arihantas (Enlightened Ones). I bow to the Siddhas (Liberated Souls). I bow to the Acharyas (Spiritual Leaders). I bow to the Upadhyayas (Teachers). I bow to all the Sadhus (Monks) in the universe. This is the Navkar Mantra — the holiest prayer in Jainism. It bows not to a person, but to spiritual qualities.
— The Navkar Mantra (Namaskar Mantra) | Most Sacred Jain Prayer
CHAPTER 05

Sacred Slokas & Prayers

Jain scriptures contain thousands of slokas (verses) full of wisdom. Here are the most important ones explained in simple English.

Ahimsa Paramo Dharmah
Meaning: Non-violence is the highest religion/duty. This is the single most important teaching of Jainism — do not harm any living being in thought, word, or action.
— Core Jain Principle
Parasparopagraho Jīvānām
Meaning: All souls render service to one another. This is the motto of Jainism found in the Tattvarthasutra. It means all living beings are interdependent — we must help, not harm, each other. This is the ecological and social foundation of Jainism.
— Tattvarthasutra 5.21 by Umaswati
Jīvo Jīvasya Jīvanam
Meaning: Life lives on life. This acknowledges the reality of the food chain while teaching minimization of harm. Even when we must eat, we should do so with minimum violence — hence Jains prefer vegetarian and vegan diets.
— Jain Scripture
Khāmemi savva-jīve, savve jīvā khamantu me | Metti me savva-bhūesu, veram majjha na kenai
Meaning: I forgive all living beings. May all living beings forgive me. I have friendship with all. I have enmity with none. This is recited during Paryushana (Jain festival of forgiveness). It is one of the most beautiful spiritual affirmations in any religion.
— Iryavahiya Sutra (Forgiveness Prayer)
Micchāmi Dukkadam
Meaning: "May all the evil that I have done be in vain / forgiven." A short but powerful phrase said to ask forgiveness from everyone — friends, strangers, enemies — at the end of Paryushana. Jains send this message to all people in their lives.
— Paryushana Greeting
Ārambhajā Parigrahe Rāgadveshau Cha Hetavah | Kāranam Bandhasya Proktam Mokshasy Āpi Viparyayah
Meaning: Violence in worldly activities, possessiveness, attachment and hatred — these are the causes of karmic bondage. And their opposites are the causes of liberation. This explains Jain ethics in one verse: let go of greed, anger, and attachment to be free.
— Tattvarthasutra
Ātmā Vā Are Drastam Śrotavyam Mantavyam Nididhyāsitavyah
Meaning: The Self is to be seen, heard, reflected upon, and meditated upon. Know yourself — this is the beginning and end of Jain spiritual practice. The path to liberation starts with deep understanding of one's own soul.
— Jain Philosophy
Savve Jīvā Vi Icchanti, Jīvium Na Mariyum | Tasmā Pānam Na Hantavvam, Naccā Hantavvam
Meaning: All living beings desire to live, not to die. Therefore, do not kill any being, nor cause any being to be killed. This Prakrit verse from Mahavira's teachings forms the philosophical basis for vegetarianism, animal rights, and compassion in Jain ethics.
— Acharanga Sutra (Words of Mahavira)
Siddham, Siddham, Siddham | Namo Arihantānam | Namo Siddhānam
Meaning: Perfection, Perfection, Perfection! I salute the Arihantas! I salute the Siddhas! A victory chant celebrating spiritual perfection — used in Jain worship (Puja) and meditation. It affirms that liberation is possible and beautiful.
— Jain Vandana (Worship Verse)
Chaitanya-Shakti Svabhāvo Ātmā
Meaning: The nature of the soul is pure consciousness. This concise verse from Jain metaphysics explains what you truly are at the deepest level — not your body, not your mind, not your name or role — but pure, luminous, eternal awareness.
— Samayasara by Kundakunda
Uppannei Vā Vigamei Vā Dhuvei Vā
Meaning: Things arise, pass away, and persist. This is the Jain theory of reality — existence has three aspects: origination, destruction, and permanence. Everything changes (moments arise and pass), yet something essential persists. Similar to Buddhist impermanence but with an eternal soul.
— Tattvarthasutra 5.29
Syāt Asti | Syāt Nāsti | Syāt Asti-Nāsti | Syāt Avaktavyam
Meaning: Perhaps it is | Perhaps it is not | Perhaps it both is and is not | Perhaps it is indescribable. This is the Saptabhangi — the seven-fold predication of Jain logic. Truth is relative to perspective. Nothing can be described absolutely — this teaches intellectual humility and tolerance.
— Syādvāda (Jain Theory of Conditional Predication)

CHAPTER 07

Core Philosophical Principles

🌀

ANEKANTAVADA

Many-sidedness of Truth. Reality has infinite aspects. No single viewpoint is the complete truth. Jains believe all valid perspectives deserve respect — this gave rise to tolerance and pluralism centuries before modern democracy.

🔗

KARMA THEORY

Karma is Physical in Jainism! Unlike other traditions where karma is abstract, Jains believe karma is actual fine particles that stick to the soul based on actions, thoughts, and emotions. Purity removes them.

💎

SOUL (JIVA)

Every living being has an eternal soul (Jiva). The soul is pure consciousness with unlimited knowledge, power, and bliss — but karma clouds these. Liberation means the soul returns to its pure state.

🌍

NO CREATOR GOD

Jainism does not believe in a creator God who made the universe. The universe is eternal and self-sustaining. What Jains worship are perfected souls (Arihantas and Siddhas) as role models for liberation.

🔄

SAMSARA

The cycle of birth, life, death, and rebirth. The soul is reborn in different forms — human, animal, plant, divine, hellish — based on karma. The goal of Jain practice is to break free from this cycle.

🏔️

MOKSHA

Final liberation. When all karma is removed, the soul rises to the top of the universe (Siddhashila) and remains there forever in pure bliss, knowledge, and peace. This is the ultimate goal of a Jain life.

🌿

TATTVARTHASUTRA

The most authoritative Jain scripture, written by Umaswati (~2nd CE). Contains the complete Jain worldview in 357 aphorisms covering soul, karma, cosmos, ethics, and liberation — accepted by both major sects.

🕯️

THREE JEWELS

Right Faith (Samyak Darshana), Right Knowledge (Samyak Jnana), and Right Conduct (Samyak Charitra) — the three-fold path to liberation. All three are needed together.

⚗️

SYADVADA

Every statement is conditionally true. "Maybe this is so; maybe it is not so; maybe it is indescribable." This system of logic prevents dogmatism and promotes intellectual humility — radical for its time.

CHAPTER 08

Karma & the Soul — The Jain View

What is Karma in Jainism?

Unlike most traditions, Jainism treats karma as actual material particles (pudgala — matter). These invisible particles float throughout the universe. When you act with passion (anger, greed, desire, ego), these particles stick to your soul like dust sticks to a wet cloth.

The soul then becomes "dirty" — its natural qualities of infinite knowledge, vision, power, and bliss get covered up. This covering is what makes us ignorant, limited, and suffering.

8 Types of Karma

  • 🔵 Jnanavaraniya — Obstructs knowledge
  • 🔵 Darshanavaraniya — Obstructs perception
  • 🔴 Vedaniya — Creates pleasure and pain
  • 🔴 Mohaniya — Delusion karma (most dangerous)
  • 🟡 Ayushya — Determines lifespan
  • 🟡 Namakarma — Determines body type
  • 🟢 Gotrakarma — Determines social status
  • 🟢 Antaraya — Obstacles to gain, giving, enjoyment

How to Remove Karma

The two-part process: stop new karma (Samvara) and burn away old karma (Nirjara).

Samvaro Nirjarā Mokshah
Meaning: Stopping karma (Samvara) + Shedding karma (Nirjara) = Liberation (Moksha). This equation summarizes the entire Jain spiritual practice.
— Tattvarthasutra

Ways to Shed Karma

🔥 Tapas (Austerity) — Fasting, meditation, physical discipline
🙏 Prayaschitta — Confession and repentance
🧘 Samadhi — Deep meditative equanimity
💧 Vinaya — Humility and reverence
🌿 Vairagya — Detachment from worldly things
📖 Svadhyaya — Study of scriptures

CHAPTER 09

Anekantavada — The Philosophy of Many Truths

🐘 The Blind Men and the Elephant

Six blind men touch an elephant. One feels the trunk and says "It's a snake!" Another touches a leg and says "It's a tree!" Another feels the side and says "It's a wall!" — Each is partially right, but none has the full truth. This famous parable is used in Jainism to explain why no single viewpoint is complete. All partial truths deserve respect.

Anekantavada (many-sidedness) is perhaps Jainism's greatest gift to world philosophy. It says:

🔍

NAYA (VIEWPOINT)

Every perspective is a partial truth. There are 7 primary ways to view any reality — each captures one true aspect but not the whole.

🗣️

SYADVADA (CONDITIONAL)

All assertions must be prefixed with "Syat" (maybe/perhaps). This prevents dogmatic claims. Every statement is true from some perspective.

🤐

AVAKTAVYA (INEFFABLE)

Some things cannot be described in words at all. Language is limited. Ultimate truth transcends all description.

Syāt Asti ca Nāsti ca Avaktavyam ca — Saptabhangi
The Seven Predicates (Saptabhangi):
1. Syāt Asti — Maybe it exists
2. Syāt Nāsti — Maybe it doesn't exist
3. Syāt Asti Nāsti — Maybe it both exists and doesn't
4. Syāt Avaktavyam — Maybe it's indescribable
5. Syāt Asti Avaktavyam — Maybe it exists and is indescribable
6. Syāt Nāsti Avaktavyam — Maybe it doesn't exist and is indescribable
7. Syāt Asti Nāsti Avaktavyam — Maybe it exists, doesn't exist, and is indescribable

This is the most sophisticated logic system in ancient philosophy — remarkably similar to modern quantum mechanics' superposition states!
— Jain Epistemology
CHAPTER 10

Digambara vs Shvetambara

Despite their differences, both sects agree on all fundamental teachings of Jainism.

⬛ Digambara

"Sky-clad" — the monks wear no clothes at all, as a symbol of complete detachment from all possessions.

  • Monks are nude — the body itself is their "sky"
  • Women cannot achieve liberation in current body (must reborn as man)
  • Original scriptures (Agamas) are lost — only later texts remain
  • Concentrated mostly in South India (Karnataka, Maharashtra)
  • Famous for the Gommateshwara statue at Shravanabelagola
  • Monks eat only once a day, standing, from cupped palms

⬜ Shvetambara

"White-clad" — monks and nuns wear simple white robes as a symbol of purity and non-attachment.

  • Monks wear white robes; carry a mouth-covering cloth (muhpatti)
  • Women can achieve liberation (Mallinatha, 19th Tirthankara, was a woman)
  • Preserve 45 original Agama (canonical) scriptures
  • Concentrated in North India (Gujarat, Rajasthan, Mumbai)
  • Famous for Dilwara Temples, Palitana temples
  • More liberal — allow monks to use bowls and carry belongings
CHAPTER 11

Sacred Symbols of Jainism

☸️

THE DHARMACHAKRA

The wheel of Dharma — representing the cycle of time and cosmic order. Found on Jain flags and temples.

🤚

THE HAND (ABHAYA)

A raised hand with the word "Ahimsa" in the center and a wheel on the palm. Means "Stop! Do not harm." The most recognized Jain symbol.

🔱

THE SWASTIKA

In Jainism (3000+ years before Nazi misappropriation) the Swastika represents the 4 realms of rebirth and the 4-fold Jain community: monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen.

🌟

THREE DOTS (TILAKATRAY)

Three dots above the Swastika represent the three jewels: Right Faith, Right Knowledge, Right Conduct — the three-fold path to liberation.

🌙

THE CRESCENT & DOT

A crescent moon with a dot above the Swastika — represents Siddhashila, the realm beyond the universe where liberated souls reside in eternal peace.

🐾

OM (JAIN)

In Jainism, OM represents the 5 supreme beings: Arihanta, Ashariri, Acharya, Upajjhaya, Muni — the Panca Parameshthi (Five Supremes).

🏛️

DILWARA TEMPLES

Mount Abu, Rajasthan. Built 1031–1230 CE. Made entirely of white marble. Considered the finest example of Jain architecture in the world. Intricate marble carvings of almost impossible delicacy.

🗿

GOMMATESHWARA

Shravanabelagola, Karnataka. 57-foot monolithic granite statue of Bahubali (son of 1st Tirthankara). Built 981 CE. One of the world's tallest free-standing stone statues. Every 12 years, bathed in milk and saffron (Mahamastakabhisheka).

🕌

RANAKPUR TEMPLES

Rajasthan. Built 1444 CE. Famous for 1444 uniquely carved marble pillars — no two pillars are identical. Dedicated to Adinath (Rishabhadeva), 1st Tirthankara.

⛰️

PALITANA TEMPLES

Gujarat. 3000+ temples on Shatrunjaya Hill. The holiest Jain pilgrimage site. One must climb 3,500+ steps to reach the summit. First Tirthankara Rishabhadeva is said to have meditated here.

🌿

PAWAPURI

Bihar. Where Lord Mahavira attained Nirvana. A beautiful lotus pond with a marble temple built over the spot where Mahavira was cremated. One of the most sacred Jain pilgrimage sites.

🏔️

SAMMET SHIKHARJI

Jharkhand. 20 of the 24 Tirthankaras attained liberation here. Considered the most sacred Jain pilgrimage site (teerth) in the world. Jains are fighting to protect it from tourism development.

CHAPTER 13

Jain Festivals & Celebrations

🙏

PARYUSHANA

The holiest Jain festival. 8 days (Shvetambara) or 10 days (Digambara — called Das Lakshana). A period of intense fasting, prayer, repentance, and forgiveness. Ends with Micchami Dukkadam — asking forgiveness from all.

August–September
🪔

DIWALI (JAIN)

Jains celebrate Diwali to mark the nirvana (liberation) of Lord Mahavira (~527 BCE). Also marks the New Year according to the Jain calendar. Lamps are lit to symbolize the light of knowledge after Mahavira's liberation.

October–November
🌺

MAHAVIR JAYANTI

Birthday of Lord Mahavira, the 24th Tirthankara. Celebrated with processions, prayers, reading of Mahavira's life, and giving to charity. A national public holiday in India.

March–April
🌊

MAHAMASTAKABHISHEKA

Held every 12 years at Shravanabelagola. The 57-foot Gommateshwara statue is ceremonially bathed (abhisheka) with milk, curd, ghee, saffron, gold, and flowers. Attended by millions. A breathtaking spectacle.

Every 12 Years
📖

AKSHAYA TRITIYA

Marks the day when Rishabhadeva (1st Tirthankara) broke his one-year fast by accepting sugarcane juice from King Shreyansa. The original "parana" (breaking of fast). Jains offer sugarcane juice to monks on this day.

April–May
🕯️

KARTIKI PURNIMA

Full moon of Kartik month. Jains offer prayers, fast, and do charitable acts. Associated with spiritual milestones of several Tirthankaras. Lamps are lit near rivers and in temples.

October–November
CHAPTER 14

Daily Practices & Way of Life

Morning Prayers (Pratikraman)

Jains perform Pratikraman — a ritual of self-examination and repentance — twice daily (morning and evening). During this, they confess any harm they may have caused (knowingly or unknowingly) to any living being and pray for forgiveness.

Samayik (Meditation)

At least once daily for 48 minutes, Jains practice Samayik — complete equanimity meditation. You become equal to all beings, feel no attachment or aversion, and simply abide in pure awareness. The word Samayik means "being in the present moment."

Diet (Ahimsa Food)

Jains are vegetarian — no meat, fish, or eggs. Many Jains also avoid root vegetables (potatoes, onions, garlic, carrots) because harvesting roots destroys the entire plant and microorganisms in the soil. Some are fully vegan. Eating after sunset is avoided to prevent swallowing insects.

Puja (Worship)

Jain Puja is unique — it's not asking God for favors. It's meditation on perfection. You bathe the Jina statue with water, milk, and flowers not because the statue needs it, but because the act purifies YOUR mind. You contemplate the qualities of a liberated being and aspire to embody them.

Fasting (Tapasya)

Jains are famous for extreme voluntary fasting. Ekasana (eating once), Ayelam (only boiled water for days), and Santhara/Sallekhana — the voluntary fast unto death when the body can no longer serve the soul. This is considered a holy death, not suicide.

Charity (Dana)

Jains give generously to four types of charity: food/medicines for monks, education, protection of animals, and serving the poor. Despite being a small community, Jains run some of India's finest hospitals, schools, and animal shelters.

Uttamacharitra Dharma — Vīra Vandana
Daily affirmation: "The highest character is the highest religion." Jainism ultimately is about becoming the best version of yourself — not through ritual, but through daily acts of non-violence, honesty, compassion, and inner stillness.
— Jain Daily Affirmation
CHAPTER 15

Jainism's Gifts to the World

🕊️

MAHATMA GANDHI

Gandhi was deeply influenced by Jain philosophy — especially Ahimsa (non-violence) and Anekantavada (seeing all perspectives). His non-violent resistance movement changed world history. "Non-violence is not a weapon of the weak, it is a weapon of the strong."

🐄

ANIMAL RIGHTS

Jains established the world's first systematic animal welfare institutions — Panjrapoles (animal shelters) — thousands of years ago. Modern animal rights philosophy closely mirrors ancient Jain teachings.

🧮

MATHEMATICS & SCIENCE

Jain scholars made extraordinary contributions: Brahmagupta's predecessors worked on zero and infinity. Jain mathematician Mahavira wrote the first systematic algebra textbook in 850 CE. Jain cosmology includes concepts of infinite sets.

🌍

ECOLOGY

Jainism is the world's oldest ecological religion. Ahimsa extends to soil, water, fire, and air. Jains don't dig wells, farm, or work with fire without careful consideration of harm. The world's first environmentalists.

🏛️

DEMOCRACY & TOLERANCE

Anekantavada's principle of respecting all viewpoints inspired democratic and pluralist thought in India centuries before the modern era. Jains coexisted peacefully with Hindus, Buddhists, and Muslims throughout history.

📚

LITERATURE & ART

Jains preserved thousands of Sanskrit and Prakrit manuscripts — many would have been lost otherwise. Jain miniature painting, architecture (Dilwara, Ranakpur), and temple sculpture are among India's greatest artistic achievements.

The Ultimate Teaching

Ātma Brahma — Aham Brahmasmi of Jainism:
Appā So Paramappā
Paramappā So Appā
"The Self is the Supreme Self. The Supreme Self is the Self."

You are already divine. You are already perfect. You are already free — you just don't know it yet. The entire purpose of Jain practice is not to gain something new, but to remove the dust of karma that covers your already-perfect soul. Liberation is your nature. Non-violence is your nature. Infinite knowledge is your nature.

The path back to yourself is the path of Jainism.
— Samayasara by Kundakunda (1st–3rd CE), the greatest Jain philosophical text
